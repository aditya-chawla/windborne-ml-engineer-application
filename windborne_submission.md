## 2. Describe an ML idea you were initially excited about but later decided was wrong or unimportant. What changed your mind?

At one point I got pretty interested in whether a more structured, graph-based hyperparameter search could beat tuning methods like grid search in a meaningful way. It was fun to think about, but in practice the gains would be tiny and inconsistent. I changed my mind after seeing that better data cleaning and evaluation choices usually mattered much more.

## 3. Describe a time when you embarked on a sidequest / took initiative outside your core job responsibilities. What value did you add to your organization? What drove you to action?

At Amdocs, I worked on the quotation system that had a bottleneck in how requests were processed in the billing workflow. I took initiative to implement unsupervised clustering ML model on the backend to resolve similar cases and a GenAI-based frontend flow to make input easier. The result was about a 40% performance improvement in that workflow.

## 4. How could better accuracy fail to translate into more useful forecasts for a weather model?

A model can get a better average score and still be less useful for people. Maybe it predicts normal days a bit better, but misses the timing of a storm, the size of a rainfall event, or where the dangerous part actually lands. For weather, people care about decisions, not just cleaner metrics, so better accuracy on paper doesnt always mean better help.

## 5. Describe a time you changed how you use LLMs in your work — switching models, tools, or workflows. What did you observe that prompted the change?

I’m pretty flexible with LLM tools and tend to switch fast when something better shows up. I’ve used Cursor, Trae, Codex, and Claude Code depending on the task. What usually drives the change is simple: which tool gives me the cleanest output, best iteration speed, and least friction for the kind of work I’m doing that week.
